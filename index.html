Sushi, one of Japan's most iconic dishes, originally began as a way to preserve fish. It was stored in fermented rice, but over time, the rice began to be eaten with the fish, evolving into the fresh sushi we know today.

The kimono, Japan’s traditional garment, has intricate designs and is worn for special occasions like weddings and festivals. Its style and colors often reflect the season and the wearer's age or marital status.

Japan has over 5 million vending machines, offering everything from snacks and drinks to hot meals, umbrellas, and even fresh eggs, making them an integral part of daily life.

Traditional Japanese homes and buildings have floors made of tatami, a mat made from rice straw. These are used in various spaces, including tea rooms and ryokans, and provide a soft, natural feel to the environment.

Many of Japan’s historical castles, such as Himeji Castle, were built primarily from wood. Their elegant, multi-tiered structures blend beauty with defense, often surrounded by moats and stone walls.

The Japanese tea ceremony, known as chanoyu or sado, is a deeply spiritual and artistic ritual involving the preparation and presentation of matcha tea. It emphasizes harmony, respect, purity, and tranquility.

Mochi, a chewy rice cake, is a traditional food enjoyed during festivals like New Year’s. The pounding of rice to make mochi, called mochitsuki, is a communal activity symbolizing good fortune for the year ahead.

5-Day Tokyo Itinerary Overview

  • Day 1: Shinjuku, Meiji Shrine, Harajuku, Shibuya
  • Day 2: Ueno, Kitchen Street, Asakusa, Tokyo Skytree
  • Day 3: Japanese Gardens, Imperial Palace, Ginza, Akihabara
  • Day 4: Toyosu or Tsukiji Fish Market, Odaiba, TeamLab Planets, Tokyo Tower
  • Day 5:
    • Option 1: Chic boutiques and cafes in Nakameguro, Daikanyama, and Ebisu
    • Option 2: Upscale art & architecture in Aoyama, Omotesando, Azabudai Hills, and art museums
    • Option 3: Day trip to Hakone & Mt. Fuji
